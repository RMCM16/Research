What we learned

Part 2 · Question 01 · The overarching question
How are scan configurations used across our customers? What patterns can we identify?
Three patterns: universal entry · wrong dimension · fast drift off.
Almost every customer starts on a built-in preset scan configuration. The presets are organised by how long the scan takes (Lightweight / Balanced / Deep) rather than by the type of application being scanned, so they frequently don't match what the customer needs. Within a year, two-thirds of customers have drifted off presets onto custom configurations — and the journey is one-way.

Part 2 · Question 02
Why do people use custom configurations?
Because the defaults broke on their app.
Customers don't sit down and choose to write a custom configuration — they're forced into one when a built-in preset fails to scan their application properly. The most common triggers are single-page applications, broken authentication, and the need to apply consistent settings across hundreds of similar sites. Compliance frameworks (NIST, HIPAA, FedRAMP) also push customers towards bespoke configurations.

Part 2 · Question 03
Are the default configs perceived as bad?
Yes — they're sized along the wrong dimension.
Yes, customers think the defaults are inadequate — but for a specific, principled reason. Preset names describe scan duration (Lightweight, Balanced, Deep), not application architecture. Modern web apps need different scan strategies depending on whether they're a SPA, a multi-page site, or have session limits — and no current preset reflects that. Customers also can't see what each preset actually does, so they can't judge whether one fits.
Confidence 75%·perception is well-evidenced (Maia, Tom Law, Siemens, Priceline + 28% survey); "sized along the wrong dimension" is my framing — what customers literally voice is opacity ("doesn't tell me what's happening"), not the framing axis itself

Part 2 · Question 04
Do people think there is one best config?
No. One per archetype — auto-picked.
Customers don't believe in a single magic configuration that works for every app. They believe each type of application (SPA, multi-page, static, dynamic, with or without session limits) needs its own configuration — and they want the product to detect the application type and apply the right one automatically, rather than asking them to choose blindly. This is the strongest, most repeated solution signal across every role we spoke to.
Confidence 85%·strongest signal in the deck — independent quotes from T-Mobile, Maia, Syed, Alex Scott and Interpublic all converge on the same model; TS template doc proves the typology already exists internally; no counter-evidence found

Part 2 · Question 05
How much do users know about the site they're configuring?
Less than you'd expect — and it gets worse with scale.
Smaller customers usually know their applications well. Larger enterprises don't — security teams scan thousands of applications built by other teams and have very limited insight into how each one behaves (whether it's a SPA, what auth flow it uses, where the real content lives). The deeper expertise about which scan configuration fits which app sits with PortSwigger Tech Support, not with the customer.
Confidence 70%·direction is clear (Interpublic, Mastercard, Rob Cornes, 11/32 scope confusion); "worse with scale" is anecdotal — no measured correlation between estate size and configuration confusion in the data we have

Part 2 · Question 06
What makes users happy with scans?
The absence of configuration.
Customers are happy when scans complete reliably, the coverage looks plausible, and they didn't have to tune anything or call support. Reliability and trust matter more to them than depth of configuration or granular control. The happy customers are the ones whose apps happen to fit a preset out of the box — not the ones who have learned to configure expertly.
Confidence 70%·logic is sound and partly inferred from absence of complaints; 9/58 "smashing it" is a small sample with survivorship-bias risk — "no blockers" might mean indifference rather than delight

Implications

Three strategic moves

In priority order, sequenced by support pain not quotability

Move 01
Reframe presets along architecture
Replace "Lightweight / Balanced / Deep" with archetype-based defaults that match how customers and TS already think. Syed's template doc is the ready-made decision tree.
Move 02
Detect, don't ask
A discovery probe that classifies the site (SPA / MPA / Static / Dynamic, session-limited y/n) and routes to the right config. Solves the knowledge-gap problem at enterprise scale.
Move 03
Lead with authentication
Recorded-login resilience is the largest queue (79 cases in the last 6 months) and the #1 ranked 2026 investment ask. SPA crawl is the strategic story; auth is the right place to start shipping.
Implications Discovery synthesis
In summary
Scan out the box is the right project. The new data sharpens the sequencing — start with authentication, classify by architecture, ship the decision tree TS already runs in their heads.
Open question: Salesforce holds support symptoms, not product telemetry. We need a one-off pull on (preset_used, time_to_first_custom_config, custom_config_count_per_site, scan_outcome) sliced by archetype to measure whether Scan out the box is succeeding once shipped.
